OVERVIEW

Coccidioidomycosis (Valley Fever) is a fungal infection caught by inhaling Coccidioides spores from disturbed soil. Reported California cases have climbed to near-record highs. Because outbreaks track hydroclimatic swings—wet winters that grow the fungus, then hot, dry spells that loft its spores—we model incidence from climate signals to forecast when and where risk peaks and guide timely public health alerts. ABOUT CocciCast

By Simon Camponuri
25 June 2026

CocciCast forecasts coccidioidomycosis — Valley fever — an emerging infectious disease caused by inhaling spores of the soil-dwelling Coccidioides fungi. Reported incidence across California has risen dramatically since 2000, and in 2023 the state recorded a near-record 9,054 cases, surpassed only by the 9,093 reported in 2019. Across the April 2023–March 2024 transmission year, which captures the full seasonal rise and fall of the disease, California reported 10,519 cases — 39% higher than the same period a year earlier. The forecasts shown throughout this dashboard are built on the ensemble model described by Camponuri, Heaney, and colleagues in Emerging Infectious Diseases (Vol. 31, No. 5, May 2025).

The model is grounded in a well-documented link between Valley fever and hydroclimatic swings. Incidence rises sharply when dry years give way to wet ones: soil moisture during wet winters is thought to support fungal growth and a buildup of spores, which are then lofted into the air during the hot, dry conditions of summer and early fall. Drought preceding the rainy season may further enhance growth by killing off competing soil microbes and by altering the rodent populations that serve as a putative reservoir and nutrient source for the fungus. California's severe 2020–2022 drought, followed by an exceptionally wet 2022–2023 winter — statewide precipitation exceeding 150% of average, among the ten wettest seasons in a century — and a second wetter-than-average 2023–2024 season at 115% of the long-term average, set up conditions for continued high incidence through the 2024 transmission year.

Forecasts come from an ensemble of five candidate algorithms — four generalized linear models with varying interactions and covariates, plus one random forest — each relating monthly reported cases per census tract to climatologic and environmental predictors. Using a progressive time-series cross-validation approach, each model's skill at predicting future out-of-sample cases was measured, and each was assigned an ensemble weight proportional to the inverse of its mean out-of-sample prediction error, so better-performing models contribute more to the blend. Separate models were fit for individual counties in highly endemic regions and for groupings of counties in low-to- moderately endemic regions, capturing spatial differences in how precipitation and temperature drive incidence. Eligible predictors included season, year, soil texture, elevation, percent impervious surface, lagged total monthly precipitation, and lagged mean temperature. Within the random forest, temperature carried high importance for forecasting cases in wetter coastal regions while precipitation dominated in the drier Central Valley — consistent with wet periods driving fungal growth and hot, dry periods driving spore dispersal.

To project forward, the models were fit to data spanning January 2000–December 2022 and applied to observed temperature and precipitation through March 2024, with climate beyond the April 2024 analysis date extrapolated from PRISM data: monthly temperatures following a 42-year (1981–2023) linear trend, and precipitation set to the 50th percentile of the 42-year distribution. Because future climate is unknown, forecasts were stress-tested against drier (20th percentile) and wetter (80th percentile) precipitation scenarios and warmer (+3°F) and cooler (−3°F) temperature scenarios; results were similar across all of them, indicating that prior climate conditions matter more than concurrent weather. Monthly ensemble forecasts were formed as the weighted average of the component models, and 90% prediction intervals were generated through a two-step bootstrapping procedure. All analyses were conducted in R.

Applied retrospectively, the ensemble predicted 11,846 cases (90% PI 11,261–12,505) statewide during April 2023–March 2024, closely tracking the preliminary state report of 10,519 and reproducing the observed monthly peak of roughly 1,462 cases; the fit to historical case data was strong (R² = 0.87). Looking ahead, the model forecast 12,244 cases (90% PI 11,579–12,964) statewide during April 2024–March 2025 — a 62% increase over the transmission year two years earlier and on par with the high incidence of 2023.

The heaviest burden is concentrated in a handful of regions. For the 2024–2025 transmission year, the Southern San Joaquin Valley was projected to see the most infections (5,399 cases; 90% PI 4,993–5,902), followed by the Southern Coast (3,322; 90% PI 3,172–3,494) and the Central Coast (1,207; 90% PI 1,071–1,378), with smaller totals across the Bay Area, Northern San Joaquin Valley, Southern Inland, and other regions. The forecast shows pronounced seasonality: incidence begins climbing in June and peaks in November at about 1,411 cases statewide (90% PI 1,267–1,587) — 98% higher than the 2022 peak of 714 and nearly matching the 2023 peak.

Because reported cases typically lag exposure by one to two months, the November peak reflects exposures during the dry late-summer and fall, when drier soils generate more dust and airborne spores — though risk persists year-round. Climate change is reshaping California's hydroclimate: while average precipitation may change only modestly, its variability is expected to grow, producing larger and more frequent swings between very wet and very dry conditions — "precipitation whiplash" — exactly the pattern associated with Valley fever surges. The forecasts carry important caveats: case data are aggregated to disease onset (which lags exposure) and to census tract of residence (which may differ from where exposure occurred), future climate is inherently uncertain, and the model cannot anticipate stochastic point-source outbreaks.
